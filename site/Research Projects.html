My dissertation broadly investigates the role of topology in perception, working memory, and categorization in children. Topology is imagined as a “rubber-sheet geometry” in which the number of holes, connectivity, and inside/outside relationship of an object remain invariant through deformations like stretching, twisting, bending, and shrinking, except through gluing and tearing (see detailed review by Chen, 2005). Unlike surface features (e.g., shape, color) topological properties (like holes in objects) provides stability to the visual representations across different contexts.

Development of Perception of hole shapes

Holes act as a paradox in figure/ground organization (Palmer, 1999). Holes are massless object space but we still perceive and report the shape of holes. Kim (2020) using Bouba/Kiki phonetic sound and shape associations showed that adults can phenomenologically perceive the shape of holes. In this study, in collaboration with Dr. Melissa M Kibbe and Dr. Sung-Ho Kim, I am investigating a developmental pattern of phenomenological perception of hole shapes in 3-7-year-old children by exploiting the shape-sound correspondence of Bouba/Kiki. We presented the flower-shaped hole and spikier shape hole object and asked the children "Which one is Bouba/Kiki?". In experiment 1, we found that direct perception of hole shape develops between 3-7-years suggesting that children can assign the inner contour to the hole and therefore perceive the hole shape. We are currently collecting data for experiment 2 (control for experiment 1) and experiment 3 (functional meaning of holes by adding eyes).
